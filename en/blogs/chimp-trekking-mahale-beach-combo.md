## Introduction

As a zoology PhD, my research focuses on primate behavior. I studied chimpanzees in Kibale Forest, Uganda for 3 years, mountain gorillas in Volcanoes National Park, Rwanda for 2 years, but August 2025 in Mahale Mountains National Park still shocked me.

Mahale Mountains is Tanzania's most remote wilderness, accessible only by small plane. Home to one of world's largest wild chimpanzee populations‚Äîabout 1000 chimpanzees, divided into 8 communities.

Tracking wild chimpanzees isn't watching zoo performances, it's truly entering their world, observing their most natural behaviors. When you see a chimpanzee using stone to crack nuts, or using stick to fish for termites, you realize‚Äîwe have much more in common with them than we imagined.

## Trip Highlights

### Day 1: Arrive in Mahale - Lake Tanganyika's Blue Miracle

**9:00 AM - Small Plane Takes Off**

12-seat Cessna Caravan from Arusha to Mahale Mountains. Flight time about 2.5 hours, flying over Serengeti and Ruaha National Parks.

Pilot is Tanzanian, named Moses. He said: "Perfect weather today, you'll see Lake Tanganyika's blue miracle."

**11:30 AM - Land at Kigoma Airstrip**

Plane landed at Kigoma airstrip, then transferred by speedboat to Mahale Mountains National Park. Speedboat ride about 3 hours, enjoying Lake Tanganyika's scenery.

Lake Tanganyika is world's second-deepest freshwater lake, deepest point 1470 meters, water crystal clear, can see fish underwater.

**3:00 PM - Arrive at Greystoke Mahale Camp**

We checked into [Greystoke Mahale Camp]({{ site.baseurl }}/hotels/), Mahale's most luxurious camp, built on lakeside beach, all rooms thatched cottages facing Lake Tanganyika.

Camp manager is French, named Sophie. She smiled: "Welcome to paradise."

**5:00 PM - Lakeside Sunset**

We waited for sunset on lakeside beach. Sun setting, golden light on lake surface, distant Mahale Mountains like giant barrier.

"Lake Tanganyika is one of world's oldest lakes," Sophie said, "It's 9 million years old."

**7:00 PM - Camp Dinner**

Dinner was freshly grilled fish‚Äîfreshwater fish from Lake Tanganyika, with fresh vegetables and rice.

"Tomorrow 7 AM departure, chimpanzee tracking," Sophie said, "Need long sleeves and pants, many mosquitoes and ticks in forest."

### Day 2: First Tracking - M Group's Social Moment

**7:00 AM - Start Tracking**

Our guide was John, tracker worked in Mahale Mountains 20 years. Can distinguish every chimpanzee's call, knows their location.

"Today we look for M Group," John said, "Community of 35 chimpanzees, leader named Mzee."

We started hiking through dense tropical rainforest. Mahale Mountains vegetation very dense, many giant trees covered with vines and moss.

**9:00 AM - First See Chimpanzees**

Hiked about 2 hours, John suddenly stopped, pointed ahead: "Listen!"

We heard chimpanzee calls from distance‚Äî"pant-hoot" calls, their communication method.

"They're over there!" John excitedly.

Continued 15 minutes, finally saw first chimpanzee‚Äîfemale chimpanzee, sitting on branch, grooming fur with fingers.

"This is Mama," John said, "M Group's oldest female, about 45 years old."

**10:00 AM - Chimpanzees' "Social Moment"**

We saw more chimpanzees‚Äîat least 15, active in trees, some eating, some playing, some grooming.

"Chimpanzees highly social animals," John explained, "They have complex social structure, with leaders, hierarchy, alliances."

We saw Mzee‚ÄîM Group's male leader, strong adult male, much larger than other chimpanzees. Sitting on branch, vigilantly watching surroundings.

**11:00 AM - Chimpanzees Using Tools**

We saw young male chimpanzee using stone to crack nuts. Placed nut on flat stone, used another stone to crack.

"This is chimpanzees' most famous behavior‚Äîusing tools," John said, "Jane Goodall first discovered this in Gombe."

I held breath, recording this precious moment with camera. Chimpanzee successfully cracked nut, ate kernel inside, then placed stone aside for next use.

**1:00 PM - Return to Camp**

Reluctantly left forest, returned to camp. Although only tracked 3 hours, already saw chimpanzees' most natural behaviors‚Äîsocial, grooming, tool use.

**3:00 PM - Lake Tanganyika Swimming**

Afternoon, we swam in Lake Tanganyika. Water crystal clear, temperature moderate, very comfortable.

"Lake Tanganyika's water from underground springs and rain," Sophie said, "One of world's cleanest lakes."

We swam, snorkeled, saw many beautiful fish‚Äîcichlids, catfish, bass.

**7:00 PM - Camp Lecture**

John gave lecture about chimpanzees. Talked about chimpanzee social structure, behavior patterns, conservation status.

"Mahale Mountains' chimpanzee population very precious," John said, "One of world's most endangered primates, fewer than 150,000 remain globally."

---

### Day 3: Second Tracking - Hunting Shock

**7:00 AM - Track Again**

Today we looked for another community‚ÄîK Group, community of 28 chimpanzees.

"K Group famous for hunting," John said, "They often hunt monkeys, especially red colobus."

**9:00 AM - Tracking Starts**

Hiked 1.5 hours, finally found K Group. Active in forest, scattered in different trees.

**10:00 AM - Hunting Shock**

Suddenly, we heard chimpanzee screams‚Äînot "pant-hoot" calls, but sharper screams.

"They're hunting!" John whispered.

We saw group of chimpanzees chasing red colobus monkey. Monkey jumping between trees, trying to escape, but chimpanzees coordinated, surrounding from different directions.

Entire process less than 1 minute, chimpanzees caught monkey. Dragged it under tree, started sharing prey.

"This is chimpanzees' most shocking behavior," John said, "Only primates that hunt cooperatively."

I held breath, recording this cruel but magnificent scene. Not cruelty, this is natural selection.

**11:00 AM - Sharing Prey**

Chimpanzees shared prey‚Äîmale leader first, then other males, finally females and cubs.

"Sharing prey important behavior in chimpanzee society," John explained, "Strengthens community cohesion."

**1:00 PM - Return to Camp**

Returned to camp, mixed emotions. Saw chimpanzees' most shocking behavior‚Äîhunting, but this made us understand natural selection more deeply.

**3:00 PM - Lakeside Meditation**

Afternoon, we meditated on lakeside beach. Lake Tanganyika's blue water, distant Mahale Mountains, dense tropical rainforest, all brought peace.

"This is Africa," John said, "Life, birth, hunting, escape, all happening simultaneously."

---

### Day 4: Final Tracking - Cubs' Learning

**7:00 AM - Final Tracking**

Final day, we looked for M Group again. John said: "Today focus on observing cubs' learning process."

**9:00 AM - Cubs' Learning**

We saw many cubs‚Äîat least 10, ages from months to years. Playing, learning, imitating adult chimpanzees.

"Cubs' learning process very critical," John explained, "They must learn survival skills in shortest time‚Äîhunting, foraging, social."

We saw cub imitating adult chimpanzee using stick to fish termites‚Äîinserted stick into termite mound, pulled out, licked termites off. Technique not skilled yet, but very serious.

**10:00 AM - Motherhood's Greatness**

We saw female chimpanzee caring for her cub‚Äîcub sleeping in her arms, she gently grooming cub's fur with fingers.

"Chimpanzee motherhood very great," John said, "Mother cares for cub at least 5 years, teaches survival skills."

**11:00 AM - Farewell**

Reluctantly said goodbye to chimpanzees, returned to camp. These 3 days of tracking made me rediscover primate intelligence and emotion.

**3:00 PM - Final Lake Tanganyika Swim**

Afternoon, final swim in Lake Tanganyika. Water crystal clear, temperature moderate, very comfortable.

"I'll miss this place," John said, "Mahale Mountains is my home."

**7:00 PM - Farewell Dinner**

Dinner was roasted whole lamb‚Äîlamb from local farm, with fresh vegetables and rice.

"Tomorrow you leave," Sophie said, "Hope you'll come back."

### üéí Packing Recommendations

**Essential Gear**:
- Long sleeves and pants (mosquito/tick protection)
- Waterproof boots (forest very wet)
- Sunscreen and hat
- Lightweight jacket (forest cool)
- Binoculars (8x42 or 10x42)
- Camera + telephoto lens (at least 200mm)
- Power bank (camp no electricity)
- Wet wipes

**Optional Items**:
- Recording device (record chimpanzee calls)
- Rain cover (protect equipment)
- Mosquito repellent (very important!)
- Tick spray (very important!)
- Water bottle (need hydration during tracking)

### üí° Tips

1. **Best time**: June-October is dry season, forest relatively dry, tracking easier. December-March is rainy season, forest very wet, tracking more difficult.

2. **Best location**: Mahale Mountains National Park is best location for chimpanzee tracking, has largest wild chimpanzee population.

3. **Tracking rules**: Must maintain 7-meter distance during tracking, don't feed, don't use flash, don't make loud noise.

4. **Physical preparation**: Tracking requires 2-4 hours hiking, forest terrain complex, need certain fitness.

5. **Safety first**: Chimpanzees wild animals, potentially aggressive, follow guide's advice.

6. **Respect nature**: Don't disturb chimpanzees, don't destroy their habitats, don't take anything.

7. **Record everything**: Photograph, video, write diary, these records valuable for scientific research.
