## Overview

The largest land animal on Earth today. Highly intelligent, possessing self-awareness, able to recognize themselves in mirrors. Female elephants live together for life, led by the oldest female (matriarch). Remarkable memory, able to remember water source locations from decades ago.

---

## Physical Characteristics

### Size Data
- **Weight**: Males 4000-7000kg (equivalent to 5-8 small cars), females 2000-4000kg
- **Shoulder Height**: Males 3.2-4 meters, females 2.5-3 meters
- **Body Length**: 6-7.5 meters (excluding trunk and tail)

### Unique Features
1. **Trunk**: Approximately 2 meters long, containing 40,000 muscles, can lift 300kg objects, and also pick up peanuts
2. **Tusks**: Male tusks are longer and thicker, up to 2-3 meters, weighing up to 100kg
3. **Ears**: African elephant ears are fan-shaped (Asian elephant ears are smaller), used for heat dissipation and displaying emotions
4. **Feet**: 4 toes, soles have soft pads, walk silently

---

## Behavior and Habits

### Feeding Behavior
- **Massive Appetite**: Feeds for 16 hours daily, consuming 150-300kg of plants
- **Diverse Diet**: Grass, leaves, tree bark, fruits, roots
- **Toppling Trees**: Use trunk and tusks to push down trees to access leaves
- **Drinking**: Can drink 200 liters of water at once, sucks water with trunk then sprays into mouth

### Social Structure
- **Matriarchal Society**: Led by the oldest female (matriarch, usually 50-60 years old)
- **Family Size**: 8-100 individuals, typically consisting of female relatives
- **Male Dispersal**: Males leave the family at 12-15 years, becoming solitary or joining male groups
- **Emotional Bonds**: Mothers and calves have close relationships, will care for injured or sick family members

### Intelligence and Memory
- **Exceptional Memory**: Can remember water source locations and migration routes from decades ago
- **Self-Awareness**: Can recognize themselves in mirrors (one of few animals)
- **Mourning Behavior**: Will circle around bones of deceased companions, touching with trunks, showing grief
- **Tool Use**: Use branches to scratch, use stones to crack nuts

### Communication
- **Infrasound**: Use low-frequency sounds (inaudible to humans) to communicate with companions several kilometers away
- **Body Language**: Ear position, trunk movements, body posture
- **Touch**: Use trunks to touch each other to show friendliness

## Conservation Status

### Conservation Status: **Endangered**

### Threat Factors
1. **Ivory Poaching**: 1970-1980s, African elephant population decreased from 1.3 million to 600,000
2. **Habitat Loss**: Agricultural expansion, human settlement leading to habitat fragmentation
3. **Human-Elephant Conflict**: Elephants destroy crops, killed in retaliation by humans
4. **Climate Change**: Drought intensification, reduced water sources

### Conservation Measures
- **International Ivory Trade Ban**: 1989 CITES Convention implementation
- **National Park Protection**: Tanzania established multiple protected areas
- **Anti-Poaching Patrols**: Increased patrol efforts, cracking down on poaching
- **Community Conservation**: Encourage local community participation, provide alternative livelihoods

### Population Recovery
- **Tanzania**: Recovered from 50,000 in 1980s to 120,000 today
- **Tarangire**: Has one of East Africa's largest elephant herds, approximately 3,000

## Safari Observation Tips

### Best Viewing Locations
- **Tarangire**: Largest elephant herd in East Africa, classic scene of giant baobab trees with elephants
- **Ruaha**: Largest park in Tanzania, elephant population reaches 12,000
- **Serengeti**: Central plains and southern Ndutu region
- **Ngorongoro**: Crater floor, often see elephant families

### Identification Points
- **Size**: Largest land animal, unmistakable
- **Ears**: African elephant ears are fan-shaped, Asian elephant ears are smaller
- **Tusks**: Male tusks longer and thicker, female tusks shorter
- **Behavior**: Usually appear in groups, led by older females

### Observation Timing
- **Early Morning (6-9am)**: Elephants most active, heading to water sources
- **Late Afternoon (4-7pm)**: Returning from forest to grasslands to feed
- **Avoid**: Midday (too hot, elephants hide in shade)

### Photography Tips
- **Lens**: 100-400mm, wide-angle for classic shots of elephants with baobab trees
- **Composition**: Include environment to show elephant's massive size
- **Behavior**: Photograph elephants toppling trees, drinking, mother-calf interactions
- **Patience**: Elephants move slowly, need to wait for the right moment

### Safety Tips
- **Maintain Distance**: At least 50 meters, do not approach females with calves
- **Do Not Disturb**: Do not make loud noises or sudden movements
- **Follow Guide**: Guides understand elephant behavior and safe distances best

## Interesting Facts

1. **Versatile Trunk**: Can breathe, smell, grasp, suck water, make sounds, even use to shake hands
2. **Excellent Swimmers**: Elephants can swim, can use trunk as snorkel to dive underwater
3. **Sensitive Skin**: Elephant skin is thick but sensitive to sunlight, needs to roll in mud regularly for sun protection
4. **Longevity Champions**: Wild elephants can live 60-70 years, up to 80 years in captivity
5. **Longest Gestation**: Elephant pregnancy is 22 months, longest of all mammals
6. **No Sweat Glands**: Elephants have no sweat glands, cool down by flapping ears and mud baths
